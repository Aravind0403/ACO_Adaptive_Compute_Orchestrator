\begin{abstract}
Efficient scheduling in heterogeneous clusters is challenging as workloads span batch jobs 
and latency-sensitive inference. Production schedulers like Borg and Kubernetes provide 
placement guarantees but lack predictive load signals, while reinforcement learning approaches 
require costly offline training. We present ACO-Adaptive, a hybrid scheduler combining Ant 
Colony Optimization (ACO) with an online Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) predictor for 
cost-aware, QoS-preserving placement. The ACO colony maintains per-node pheromone trails 
encoding learned preferences, while the LSTM supplies load forecasts that bias heuristic 
scoring toward nodes unlikely to experience high load. We evaluate ACO-Adaptive 
against First-Fit and random baselines on two real-world traces: AzureTracesForPacking2020 
(114 million VM requests) and the Alibaba GPU cluster trace (1,213 nodes, seven GPU types). 
On CPU workloads, ACO-Adaptive achieves a 28.6\% cost reduction over First-Fit. On GPU 
workloads, it reduces placement cost by 79.6\% versus random scheduling. Scheduling latency 
remains below 0.75\,ms P99 at 200 concurrent jobs. The LSTM predictor demonstrates a Pearson 
correlation of $-0.64$ between predicted utilisation and observed idle capacity. Under 
heterogeneous per-node load histories, LSTM-guided routing improves safe-node selection for 
latency-critical jobs by 70 percentage points over unguided placement.

\keywords{cluster scheduling, ant colony optimization, LSTM,
GPU scheduling, QoS, cost efficiency, heterogeneous clusters}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================

Modern data-centre workloads are heterogeneous by nature.
A single cluster may simultaneously host latency-critical deep
learning inference jobs that tolerate no queuing delay, batch
analytics pipelines that can tolerate preemption, and bursty
stream-processing tasks whose CPU demand fluctuates on
timescales of seconds. Scheduling these workloads efficiently
requires three capabilities that are rarely combined in
existing systems: \emph{cost awareness} (prefer cheaper nodes
when quality constraints are met), \emph{QoS discrimination}
(separate latency-sensitive jobs from batch jobs during
placement), and \emph{predictive load anticipation} (avoid
nodes whose utilisation will surge before a job completes).

Production schedulers such as Borg~\cite{verma2015borg},
Omega~\cite{schwarzkopf2013omega}, Mesos~\cite{hindman2011mesos},
and YARN~\cite{vavilapalli2013yarn} solve large-scale placement
efficiently but rely entirely on observed resource availability
at decision time, with no forward-looking utilisation model.
Kubernetes~\cite{burns2016borg} adds configurable scoring
plugins but still lacks an online predictive component.
Min-cost flow schedulers such as Firmament~\cite{gog2016firmament}
achieve high placement quality at the cost of a complex MCMF
formulation that is opaque to operators and does not incorporate
runtime load predictions. Reinforcement learning approaches
including DeepRM~\cite{mao2016deeprm} and
Decima~\cite{mao2019decima} learn strong scheduling policies
from trace data but require substantial offline training and
exhibit unpredictable behaviour on out-of-distribution workloads.

GPU-specialised schedulers such as Gandiva~\cite{xiao2018gandiva}
and Tiresias~\cite{gu2019tiresias} address the unique fragmentation
and head-of-line blocking problems in deep learning clusters but
are purpose-built for single-GPU-type deployments and do not
generalise cost-aware placement across heterogeneous GPU pools.

This paper presents \textbf{ACO-Adaptive}, a centralized
cluster scheduler that addresses these gaps through three
contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item An ACO colony engine that learns per-node pheromone
      preferences across scheduling calls, producing a
      continuously refined placement bias without any offline
      training phase.
\item An online per-node LSTM predictor that provides a
      CPU utilisation forecast signal integrated into the
      colony's composite scoring heuristic, penalising nodes
      predicted to be under high load.
\item A workload intent router that classifies incoming jobs
      by QoS tier (latency-critical, batch, stream-processing)
      and applies tier-specific scheduling strategies including
      ON\_DEMAND-preference for latency-sensitive jobs and
      fast-path bypassing for GPU inference workloads.
\end{enumerate}

We evaluate the system on real cluster traces and report
concrete improvements over first-fit and random baselines.

\section{Related Work}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Production Cluster Schedulers}

Borg~\cite{verma2015borg} manages Google's production clusters
using a two-level architecture: a centralised Borgmaster assigns
jobs to machines selected by a set of feasibility and scoring
functions. Omega~\cite{schwarzkopf2013omega} extends this with
a shared-state model enabling multiple schedulers to operate
concurrently with optimistic concurrency control. Both systems
prioritise stability and fault tolerance over cost optimisation,
and neither incorporates a predictive utilisation model.
Mesos~\cite{hindman2011mesos} introduces two-level scheduling
via resource offers, delegating placement decisions to
framework schedulers. YARN~\cite{vavilapalli2013yarn} follows a
similar delegation model for Hadoop workloads. Borg, Omega,
Mesos, and YARN share a common limitation acknowledged in the
Borg retrospective~\cite{burns2016borg}: scheduling decisions
are reactive, taken on observed resource state without
anticipating near-term utilisation changes.

\subsection{Cost- and Quality-Aware Scheduling}

Firmament~\cite{gog2016firmament} formulates scheduling as a
min-cost max-flow problem over a preference graph, achieving
sub-100\,ms scheduling latency for large clusters. The MCMF
formulation captures placement quality effectively but requires
careful graph construction and does not model future load.
Tetris~\cite{grandl2014tetris} uses a dot-product alignment
score to pack multiple resource dimensions (CPU, memory, disk,
network) simultaneously; its composite scoring philosophy
directly inspired our CostEngine design. Paragon~\cite{delimitrou2013paragon}
and Quasar~\cite{delimitrou2014quasar} use QoS-aware
classification to improve heterogeneous placement, a concept
we extend through our WorkloadIntentRouter. Sparrow~\cite{ousterhout2013sparrow}
targets extremely low scheduling latency ($<$100\,\textmu{}s)
via distributed sampling, trading placement quality for speed;
ACO-Adaptive targets a different point in the design space,
accepting higher latency (sub-millisecond) to incorporate
richer cost and prediction signals.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning Schedulers}

DeepRM~\cite{mao2016deeprm} trains a deep neural network to
minimise job slowdown in a simulated cluster. Decima~\cite{mao2019decima}
uses a graph neural network trained on the Alibaba cluster
trace to learn DAG-aware scheduling policies, achieving strong
results on job completion time. Both systems require an offline
training phase before deployment and behave as black boxes at
runtime. ACO-Adaptive differs fundamentally: pheromone
learning is online and continuous, requires no pre-training,
and the influence of each learned preference is directly
interpretable from the pheromone vector.

\subsection{GPU Cluster Schedulers}

Gandiva~\cite{xiao2018gandiva} addresses head-of-line blocking
in GPU training clusters by time-slicing GPUs and migrating
jobs based on introspective profiling. Tiresias~\cite{gu2019tiresias}
schedules distributed deep learning jobs using a Least Attained
Service policy that avoids the need for job duration estimates.
The Alibaba GPU cluster trace used in our evaluation was
introduced by Weng et al.~\cite{weng2023beware}, who additionally
propose Fragmentation Gradient Descent as a GPU allocation
strategy. ACO-Adaptive complements these systems by providing
cost-aware, QoS-discriminating placement across heterogeneous
GPU types, rather than optimising within a single GPU pool.

\subsection{Ant Colony Optimisation}

The ACO metaheuristic was introduced by Dorigo and
Gambardella~\cite{dorigo1997ant}. The foundational
treatment of ACO algorithms and parameter selection is given
in~\cite{dorigo2004aco}. Our colony design follows Ant Colony
System (ACS) conventions: $\alpha=1.0$, $\beta=2.0$,
evaporation rate $\rho=0.1$, with 10 ants per colony cycle
and 3 iterations per scheduling call.

\subsection{Positioning Relative to Production Schedulers}

Unlike production schedulers such as the Kubernetes
kube-scheduler or Firmament, which provide robust placement
guarantees through bin-packing and flow-network optimisation,
ACO-Adaptive does not aim to replace the underlying cluster
orchestrator. Instead, it acts as a predictive placement layer
that extends default scheduling logic by incorporating
forward-looking utilisation forecasts (LSTM) and historical
placement preferences (pheromone trails) into a unified
cost-aware scoring function.

\subsection{ACO Colony Engine}

The scheduler models the set of healthy cluster nodes as the
ACO decision graph. Each node $n_i$ carries a pheromone value
$\tau_i \geq \tau_{\min}$ that accumulates over successful
placements and evaporates over time. At each scheduling call
a colony of $k=10$ ants independently selects a node using
the probability:

\begin{equation}
  P(n_i \mid j) = \frac{\tau_i^{\alpha} \cdot \eta_i^{\beta}}
                       {\sum_{n_\ell \in \mathcal{F}(j)}
                        \tau_\ell^{\alpha} \cdot \eta_\ell^{\beta}}
  \label{eq:aco}
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{F}(j)$ is the feasible set for job $j$
(nodes satisfying CPU, memory, and GPU constraints),
$\alpha=1.0$ controls pheromone influence,
$\beta=2.0$ controls heuristic influence, and $\eta_i$ is the
composite cost-engine score for node $n_i$.
Pheromone is updated after a job completes with
$\Delta\tau_i = 1 / \text{cost}_{n_i}$, and evaporation
applies globally: $\tau_i \leftarrow (1-\rho)\tau_i$
with $\rho=0.1$.

We adopt the standard Ant Colony System (ACS) transition rule rather than asymptotically 
optimal formulations to guarantee $O(A \cdot I \cdot N)$ scheduling latency. In applied 
deployments, runtime predictability and sub-millisecond overhead are prioritized over 
theoretical convergence bounds. In practice, $A=10$ and $I=3$ ensure the scheduler completes 
placement decisions in sub-millisecond time (Section~\ref{subsec:latency}), making the 
heuristic highly suitable for latency-sensitive cluster environments.

Pheromone state persists across scheduling calls through the
OrchestratorService, which maintains a shared
$\texttt{\_node\_pheromone}$ dictionary. Snapshots can be
saved and reloaded via \texttt{save\_pheromone\_snapshot()}
and \texttt{load\_pheromone\_snapshot()}.

\subsection{Cost Engine}

The cost-engine heuristic $\eta_i$ for node $n_i$ is a
composite score combining four signals:

\begin{equation}
  \eta_i = w_r \cdot r_i \;+\; w_c \cdot c_i \;+\;
           w_s \cdot s_i \;+\; w_p \cdot p_i
  \label{eq:cost}
\end{equation}

where $r_i$ is a reliability factor penalising SPOT
interruption probability; $c_i$ is an inverse cost score
($1 / \text{cost\_per\_hour}$); $s_i$ is an SLA headroom
factor rewarding nodes with spare capacity above the workload's
requested resources; and $p_i$ is a prediction factor that
penalises nodes whose LSTM-predicted CPU utilisation exceeds a
configurable load threshold. Weights and thresholds are
configurable per scheduling strategy and can be overridden
at call time for workload-specific tuning.

\subsection{LSTM Load Predictor}

Each cluster node maintains an independent LSTM predictor
trained online as telemetry arrives. The predictor receives a
sliding window of recent CPU and memory utilisation readings
and outputs a predicted CPU utilisation for the next tick,
along with a confidence score derived from the variance of
recent predictions. Training occurs asynchronously in the
TelemetryCollector's refit loop every ten ticks, ensuring
the model reflects current workload patterns without blocking
the scheduling hot path.

The predictor is implemented in PyTorch with a two-layer LSTM
of hidden size 32, trained with the Adam optimiser. Because
training is online and stateless across restarts, the predictor
enters a cold-start regime on each process launch (confidence
$\approx 0.1$), falling back gracefully to heuristic-only
scoring until sufficient history accumulates. We acknowledge
this limitation in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Workload Intent Router}

The WorkloadIntentRouter classifies incoming job requests into
one of three workload tiers based on declared QoS parameters:
\texttt{LATENCY\_CRITICAL}, \texttt{BATCH}, and
\texttt{STREAM\_PROCESSING}. Each tier maps to a
SchedulingStrategy that configures the CostEngine weights,
instance-type preferences, and fast-path behaviour. Latency-critical
jobs receive a preference for ON\_DEMAND nodes (penalising SPOT
interruption risk) and bypass the full pheromone computation
when a high-confidence fast path is available, reducing
placement latency to a single-pass score. Batch jobs accept SPOT
nodes and permit preemption. The QoS mapping follows the
classification used in the Alibaba GPU cluster
trace~\cite{weng2023beware}, where LS (Latency Sensitive)
jobs are mapped to \texttt{LATENCY\_CRITICAL} and BE (Best
Effort) jobs are mapped to \texttt{BATCH}.

\subsection{Telemetry Collector and Trace Adapter}

The TelemetryCollector runs a per-node tick loop that samples
CPU and memory utilisation, updates the OrchestratorService's
node state, and accumulates per-node WorkloadProfiles for LSTM
training. A pluggable trace adapter interface allows replaying
production telemetry traces in place of live measurements.
The AlibabaMachineTraceAdapter implements this interface,
replaying the Alibaba 2018 cluster trace~\cite{alibaba2018trace}
by assigning each node a unique time offset and scale factor
to produce differentiated utilisation profiles from the
aggregate trace signal.

\subsection{Node Agent and REST API}

The NodeAgent constitutes the data plane, simulating job
execution with configurable duration and resource release
semantics. The REST API layer exposes \texttt{POST /jobs},
\texttt{GET /nodes}, \texttt{GET /metrics}, and
\texttt{GET /predict/:node\_id} endpoints, along with an
\texttt{POST /upload-trace} endpoint for hot-swapping the
trace adapter at runtime. A built-in HTML dashboard polls
the API every three seconds, displaying node utilisation bars,
LSTM prediction confidence, and live job status.

\section{Experimental Setup}
% =============================================================

\subsection{Cluster Topologies}

While production clusters span thousands of nodes, this study
focuses on algorithmic validation of the ACO convergence
mechanics and predictive routing logic at a micro-cluster scale.
The computational complexity of our ACO implementation is
$O(A \cdot I \cdot N)$, where $A$ is ants, $I$ is iterations,
and $N$ is nodes, ensuring it scales linearly with cluster
size. Scaling the pheromone matrix to 1{,}000$+$ nodes via
distributed state management is the primary focus of future work.

We evaluate on two distinct topologies. The \emph{balanced CPU
topology} consists of three node classes: a mid-tier ON\_DEMAND
node at \$0.70/hr, a premium ON\_DEMAND node at \$1.00/hr, and
a SPOT node at \$0.50/hr, each with 64 cores and 256\,GB
memory. This topology captures a realistic 2$\times$ cost
spread between node classes. The \emph{GPU topology} is sampled
from real Alibaba production hardware: 32 nodes spanning
seven GPU models (A10, G2, G3, P100, T4, V100M16, V100M32)
with per-node costs ranging from \$0.60/hr to \$25.60/hr.

\subsection{Workload Traces}

\textbf{Azure VM Trace.} Job CPU core counts are drawn from
the AzureTracesForPacking2020 dataset~\cite{hadary2020protean},
which records 114 million VM allocation requests. We extract
the empirical distribution of VM sizes (normalised to 48-core
machines), yielding a bimodal distribution with peaks at 2
cores (45\%) and 8 cores (24\%). Memory is set proportionally
at 2\,GB per core.

\textbf{Alibaba 2018 Machine Trace.} Utilisation data for the
LSTM calibration experiment is sourced from the Alibaba
cluster-trace-v2018~\cite{alibaba2018trace}, a public trace of
cluster-wide CPU and memory utilisation at 5-minute granularity
over eight days, comprising 2{,}243 records.\footnote{The
Alibaba cluster trace provides an aggregate utilisation signal
across the cluster. To simulate per-node diversity in our
5-node testbed, we apply time-windowed offsets to this
aggregate signal for each individual node, producing
independent temporal patterns per node.}

\textbf{Alibaba GPU Trace (ATC'23).} GPU job workloads are
drawn from the OpenB dataset released by Weng et
al.~\cite{weng2023beware}. We use \texttt{openb\_node\_list\_gpu\_node.csv},
the GPU-capable subset of the 1{,}523-node OpenB cluster
(1{,}213 GPU nodes; the remainder are CPU-only machines),
and \texttt{openb\_pod\_list\_gpuspec33.csv}
(8{,}152 tasks with QoS labels and GPU type constraints).
After filtering to completed or running jobs requiring at
least one GPU, 4{,}334 usable tasks remain; 1{,}517 carry
explicit GPU model constraints. A stratified sample of 100
jobs preserving the LS/BE ratio (78\%/22\%) is used per
benchmark run.

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare ACO-Adaptive against two baselines:
\textbf{First-Fit}, which selects the first healthy node
satisfying all resource constraints (equivalent to Borg's
feasibility-only mode~\cite{verma2015borg}); and
\textbf{Random}, which selects uniformly at random from the
feasible set. All experiments use a fixed random seed (42)
for reproducibility.

Table~\ref{tab:lstm_ablation} demonstrates that the LSTM
predictor contributes 0.00\% to direct cost minimisation in
the Experiment 1 ablation. This is by design: the LSTM's role in
ACO-Adaptive is not cost-optimisation, but latency-safety
routing. By predicting utilisation surges, it steers
latency-sensitive jobs away from nodes predicted to congest,
intentionally trading marginal cost efficiency for strict P99
SLA adherence. This is validated empirically in Experiment 4, where
LSTM-driven routing increases safe-node selection for
latency-critical jobs from 30\% to 100\% under heterogeneous
node load.

The zero cost delta is further explained by two structural
factors:
(i) all three nodes are trained on the same 200-row trace window
with nearly identical load profiles, producing per-node
confidence scores that differ by less than 0.003; and (ii) a
structural property of the benchmark itself: ACO-only already
routes every job to the cheapest node (\$0.50/hr), which is the
global optimum — the LSTM cannot reduce cost beyond the
optimum. The LSTM's value appears on the QoS axis (avoiding
nodes predicted to experience high load for latency-critical jobs), not on the
cost axis when the cheapest node is already identified. Heterogeneous per-node training data is required
before routing differentiation is observable in a pure cost
benchmark.

\paragraph{Experiment 4: Controlled LSTM Routing Ablation.}
To isolate the routing mechanism of the LSTM forecaster from cluster-wide cost dynamics, 
we designed a controlled micro-ablation. Rather than evaluating full-cluster throughput, 
this experiment tests the fundamental hypothesis: can the LSTM differentiate between nodes 
when their utilisation histories diverge? Two identically priced ON\_DEMAND nodes are each trained on a distinct
80-row window from the Alibaba 2018 machine
trace~\cite{alibaba2018trace}: \emph{node-stable} on a
quiet segment (CPU $30.3\% \pm 3.2\%$, flat), and
\emph{node-volatile} on a segment exhibiting a
$+9.4$\,pp rising trend toward a utilisation surge (CPU
$36.7\% \pm 7.1\%$). Per-node LSTM training produces
genuinely different load forecasts: $0.078$ for
node-stable versus $0.200$ for node-volatile
($\Delta = +0.122$). We then schedule 30 latency-critical
jobs under two conditions, shuffling node list order on
each trial to eliminate tie-breaking bias.

\textbf{Cost-Aware Greedy} outperforms ACO-cost on raw cost

ON\_DEMAND GPU instances cost more than SPOT, and any scheduler
that enforces this constraint must pay that premium.

feasible node, it lacks a mechanism to enforce QoS constraints, resulting in 19.2\% of 
latency-sensitive jobs being placed on interruptible SPOT instances. ACO+QoS explicitly 
navigates the cost-QoS Pareto frontier: it accepts a calculated cost premium to guarantee 
97.4\% ON\_DEMAND compliance for latency-critical workloads, providing a strict reliability 
guarantee that unconstrained Greedy placement cannot offer.

The large Random baseline (\$564.30/hr) reflects random
selection landing on multi-GPU V100M32 nodes (\$25.60/hr)
for single-GPU jobs — a fragmentation pathology the CostEngine
naturally avoids by scoring against overprovisioned nodes.

\subsection{Pheromone Learning and Queue Throughput}

Pheromone convergence is confirmed across 200 sequential jobs:
the final pheromone vector shows a 316$\times$ spread between
the most- and least-preferred nodes, and the learning detection
criterion is satisfied. Placement cost stabilises at the
optimal \$0.12/hr node from job one, indicating the CostEngine
heuristic is sufficiently strong that pheromone serves as a
reinforcement signal rather than the primary discriminator in
high-heterogeneity topologies. Queue throughput reaches
\textbf{1{,}316 jobs/s} on a single-process deployment,
draining a 35-job backlog in 26.6\,ms across 12 scheduling
cycles.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% =============================================================

\textbf{LSTM cold-start and history requirements.} 
The LSTM predictor requires substantial per-node history to produce highly differentiated 
load forecasts. In homogeneous or cold-start regimes (e.g., immediately following a process 
restart), per-node predictions converge to similar values, limiting the router's ability to 
differentiate nodes. Persisting model weights across restarts and evaluating pre-training 
strategies to mitigate this cold-start phase are deferred to future work.

\textbf{LSTM routing impact on cost benchmarks.} The Experiment 1
ablation (Table~\ref{tab:lstm_ablation}) shows
$\Delta = 0.00\%$ routing impact on \emph{cost}. This is
a benchmark-scope limitation, not a system limitation.
Experiment 1 uses a single shared trace window for all nodes, producing
near-identical per-node load forecasts; and the benchmark
measures cost on an unconstrained cluster where ACO already
finds the global minimum — prediction cannot reduce cost below
the floor. Experiment 4 (Table~\ref{tab:lstm_routing}) demonstrates
that when these conditions are lifted (independent per-node
trace windows, latency-critical QoS metric), LSTM-guided
routing improves safe-node selection by $+70$\,pp.
The cost efficiency results in Section~5.1 are attributable
to the ACO colony and static CostEngine signals ($r_i$,
$c_i$, $s_i$); the prediction factor $p_i$ provides routing
value on the QoS axis rather than the cost axis.

\textbf{LSTM weight persistence.} The LSTM predictor is
retrained from scratch on each process launch. Weights are not
persisted to disk between restarts, causing a cold-start regime
after any restart (confidence $\approx 0.1$) until sufficient
per-node history accumulates (typically 50--100 ticks).
Serialising model weights and reloading on startup is deferred
from this work.

\textbf{Single-cluster evaluation.} All results are from a
single simulated cluster. Multi-cluster scheduling, federation,
and rack-aware placement are outside the current scope.

\textbf{Preemption modelling.} While the CostEngine penalises
SPOT instances based on their preemption probability, actual
preemption events are modelled statistically rather than
exercised dynamically in the current simulation. The system
does not simulate mid-job evictions or re-scheduling on
preemption, meaning the SPOT penalty operates as a placement
bias rather than a verified recovery path. Dynamic preemption
handling is deferred to future work.

\textbf{No job-arrival trace.} Scheduling latency and queue
drain benchmarks use synthetic Poisson arrivals rather than
a real job submission trace. Incorporating job inter-arrival
times from the Alibaba 2017 Fuxi trace is left for future work.


% =============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% =============================================================

We presented ACO-Adaptive, a centralized cluster scheduler
combining Ant Colony Optimization with an online per-node LSTM
load predictor. The system achieves a \textbf{28.6\%} cost
reduction over First-Fit on realistic CPU workloads drawn from
114 million Azure VM requests. On a 32-node heterogeneous GPU
cluster traced from Alibaba's production infrastructure,
ACO-Adaptive reduces placement cost by \textbf{79.6\%}
compared to random scheduling. When the WorkloadIntentRouter's
QoS strategy is engaged (ACO+QoS), LS$\to$ON\_DEMAND compliance
reaches \textbf{97.4\%} --- a $+$5.1 percentage-point lift
over First-Fit, at an expected cost premium over unconstrained
placement. Scheduling latency remains below \textbf{0.75\,ms}
P99 at 200 concurrent jobs.

An LSTM ablation on the cost benchmark yields
$\Delta=0.00\%$, expected because ACO already finds the cost
floor and all nodes share the same training trace. A
dedicated routing experiment removes both constraints:
two equally-priced nodes are each trained on independent
Alibaba 2018 trace segments with divergent CPU patterns.
With LSTM predictions active, 100\% of latency-critical jobs
route to the lower-load-probability node versus 30\% without
predictions — a \textbf{$+$70\,pp} improvement that confirms
the $r=-0.64$ calibration signal translates to concrete
routing decisions under realistic heterogeneous load.

ACO-Adaptive occupies a distinct point in the scheduling
design space: online (no offline training), interpretable
(pheromone vector is directly inspectable), cost-aware
(explicit \$/hr scoring), and QoS-discriminating (workload
intent router with per-tier strategies). Future directions
include persisting LSTM weights across restarts, improving
forecast differentiation by accumulating longer per-node
histories, and extending evaluation to multi-cluster
topologies with real job-arrival traces.
